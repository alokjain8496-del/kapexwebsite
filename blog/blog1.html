How to Build a Secondary Suite in Calgary

What homeowners should plan before design, permits, and construction start.

March 19, 2026 Kapex Infra Team Renovation Planning

Adding a secondary suite can increase property value, create rental income, and make better use of your home. The best projects begin with a clear understanding of zoning, building code requirements, life safety expectations, and how the new suite will function for future tenants or family members.

1. Start with feasibility

Before drafting layouts, review the property’s current conditions. Ceiling height, utility access, window sizes, stair geometry, parking considerations, and separate entry opportunities can all affect cost and scope. A feasibility review helps identify whether the project is straightforward or whether structural and servicing upgrades will be needed.

2. Confirm permits and compliance

Secondary suites typically require coordinated permit planning. That may include development approvals, building permits, plumbing, electrical, and mechanical updates depending on the existing home and the final design. It is also important to plan around fire separation, egress, interconnected smoke alarms, ventilation, and heating distribution.

3. Design for durability and livability

A strong suite design balances compliance with a comfortable tenant experience. Prioritize natural light, durable finishes, storage, acoustics, moisture management, and practical circulation. A thoughtful layout can reduce change orders during construction and improve long-term performance.

4. Budget by construction phase

  • Demolition, framing, and structural modifications
  • Mechanical, plumbing, and electrical rough-ins
  • Insulation, fire separation, and sound control
  • Drywall, flooring, millwork, and final fixtures
  • Exterior entry adjustments and site work if needed

Breaking the budget into phases makes it easier to track risk allowances and prioritize upgrades where they matter most.

5. Work with a coordinated builder

Secondary suites involve multiple trades working in a confined footprint. A coordinated builder keeps the sequence organized, aligns inspections with progress, and reduces delays caused by missing information or rework. Clear schedules and communication are especially important when the main home is occupied during construction.
